📜 The Legacy

In the 1970s, the FCC formally recognized the 462.675 MHz frequency as the national emergency and traveler assistance channel. Driven by REACT and the NSEA, hundreds of repeaters were deployed across the US. It was commonly known as the "Orange Dot" channel on early CB-style radios.

⚡ Official status ended in 1999 — but the community kept it alive as the primary GMRS travel channel.

🛣️ Today’s Role for Travelers

Though no longer an FCC-mandated emergency channel, 462.675 remains the most widely monitored GMRS channel 20 for travelers. The accepted "Travel Tone" is CTCSS 141.3 Hz, which allows GMRS for travelers to filter out random interference.

🔁 Simplex vs. Repeater: On simplex, you talk and listen on the 462.675 MHz frequency. For repeaters, this is the output (listen) frequency — you transmit on 467.675 MHz (+5.0 MHz offset).

🤝 Community Etiquette

  • Monitor with CSQ: Set your receive to carrier squelch (no tone) so you never miss a call on the GMRS travel channel.
  • Transmit with 141.3 Hz: Use the Travel Tone when speaking to avoid triggering repeaters unnecessarily.
  • Ask first: Before a long call, say “Is the frequency in use?” to avoid stepping on a conversation.
  • Keep it brief: The 462.675 MHz frequency is for traveler assistance, not rag-chewing.
  • Identify yourself: Use your GMRS call sign when required by FCC rules.

🆘 How to Help in an Emergency

If you hear a distress call on GMRS channel 20, follow these steps:

  1. 1. Stop all traffic: Ask others to stand by.
  2. 2. Note location: Get the caller’s exact location, GPS coordinates, or landmarks.
  3. 3. Relay to 911: If you have cell coverage, call 911 and relay the information.
  4. 4. Stay on channel: Keep the caller calm and informed until help arrives.
  5. 5. Use satellite if needed: If you have a PLB or satellite messenger, activate it.
